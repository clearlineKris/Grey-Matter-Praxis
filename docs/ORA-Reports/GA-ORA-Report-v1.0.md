## Executive Summary

This ORA (Operator vs. Regulator Asymmetry) Report examines the unique asymmetries present in Georgia's highly restricted low-THC oil medical cannabis program. Unlike other states covered in this series (CO, MN, MI), Georgia has NOT legalized adult-use cannabis and maintains one of the most restrictive medical programs in the nation. The asymmetries here are fundamentally different—characterized by extreme regulatory restriction, limited market participation, and significant enforcement consequences for non-compliance.

---

## ⚠️ CRITICAL CONTEXT

**Georgia's program is NOT comparable to CO, MN, or MI:**
- Only low-THC oil (≤5% THC, ≥CBD) is legal
- No smokable, vaporizable, or edible cannabis
- Limited number of licenses issued statewide
- Criminal penalties for non-compliance remain severe
- Federal law provides no protection

## 1. Introduction: Asymmetry in Restricted Markets

### The Georgia Context
Georgia's cannabis landscape is defined by restriction, not permission:

- **Ultra-narrow product scope** — Only oil with strict THC/CBD ratios
- **Limited licensure** — Very few production and dispensary licenses
- **Criminal framework intact** — Non-program cannabis remains fully criminal
- **Nascent implementation** — Program still operationalizing

### Definition in Restricted Context
**Operator vs. Regulator Asymmetry (ORA)** in Georgia refers to imbalances that are magnified by:
- Severe consequences for any compliance failure
- Limited market participation (fewer peers to share experience)
- Nascent regulatory infrastructure
- Dual state-federal enforcement environment

## 2. Structural Asymmetries

### 2.1 Extreme Regulatory Authority

| Dimension | Regulator (GAMCC/DPH) | Operator |
|-----------|----------------------|----------|
| **Mandate** | Highly restricted program implementation | Operation within narrow bounds |
| **Flexibility** | Interpretation discretion on all matters | Zero tolerance for expansion beyond rules |
| **Consequences** | Administrative/civil focus | Criminal exposure for any misstep |
| **Scope** | Program design authority | Must accept program as designed |

### 2.2 Criminal Law Backdrop

**Unique Georgia Asymmetry:**

Unlike CO, MN, or MI where operators face primarily administrative risk, Georgia operators face:
- **Criminal prosecution** for products outside narrow definition
- **Felony charges** for unlicensed activity
- **No affirmative defense** outside registry protection
- **Law enforcement encounters** without registry card = full criminal exposure

### 2.3 Federal-State Conflict Intensified

**Asymmetry Impact:**
- State registry provides NO federal protection
- Federal employees/contractors have NO protection
- DOT-regulated workers face employment consequences
- Banking remains severely restricted
- Interstate commerce completely prohibited

## 3. Information Asymmetries

### 3.1 Nascent Program Information Gaps

| Information Type | Regulator Access | Operator Access |
|-----------------|------------------|-----------------|
| Program data | Full visibility | Very limited (few operators) |
| Enforcement precedents | Internal records | Almost none publicly |
| Patient demographics | Registry access | Anonymized only |
| Product outcomes | Testing/feedback data | Own experience only |

### 3.2 Limited Peer Information

**Unique to Georgia:**
- Very few licensed operators means minimal peer experience to draw from
- No industry associations with meaningful membership
- No established compliance benchmarks
- Each operator effectively operates in isolation

### 3.3 Legislative Information Asymmetry

**Issue:** Program expansion depends entirely on legislature, not regulator.

**Implications:**
- GAMCC cannot expand product types or conditions
- Operators must monitor legislative process directly
- Political intelligence becomes compliance intelligence
- Advocacy asymmetry (well-resourced interests vs. patients/operators)

## 4. Enforcement Asymmetries

### 4.1 Binary Compliance Environment

**Georgia's Enforcement Reality:**

Unlike graduated enforcement in mature markets, Georgia presents:

| Compliance Status | Outcome |
|-------------------|---------|
| **Within program** | Protected by registry |
| **Outside program** | Full criminal exposure |

There is minimal middle ground—compliance is largely binary.

### 4.2 Law Enforcement Encounter Dynamics

**Asymmetry Issue:** Field encounters create immediate asymmetry.

**Scenario Dynamics:**
- Officer discretion on whether to verify registry
- Registry card must be physically present
- Officer interpretation of "low-THC oil" vs. other products
- Federal officers not bound by state registry

### 4.3 Prosecution Discretion

**District Attorney Asymmetry:**
- Prosecutorial discretion on registry verification failures
- No statewide prosecution standards for edge cases
- County-by-county variation possible
- Plea bargaining leverage in any ambiguous cases

## 8. Case Studies

### Case Study 1: Law Enforcement Encounter Without Card

**Scenario:** Registered patient in traffic stop without card present.

**Asymmetry Dynamics:**
- Officer cannot verify registry on scene (potentially)
- Patient claims registration but cannot prove
- Product (oil) confiscated
- Criminal citation issued pending verification
- Patient must prove registration after-the-fact

**Outcome:** Charges dismissed upon registry verification; 6 weeks to resolution

**Asymmetry Lesson:** Patient bears burden of proof in field encounters; card must be present.

---

### Case Study 2: Product Form Ambiguity

**Scenario:** Operator produces "gel capsule" product; enforcement questions if it's an "edible."

**Asymmetry Dynamics:**
- Statute permits "gels" but not "edibles"
- Product contains low-THC oil in gel capsule format
- GAMCC interpretation requested
- Product sales halted pending guidance
- No clear precedent to reference

**Outcome:** GAMCC confirms gel capsule format is permissible; 3-month delay

**Asymmetry Lesson:** Product innovation requires pre-approval in ambiguous zones.

---

### Case Study 3: Federal Employee Patient

**Scenario:** Federal employee obtains Georgia registry card; random drug test.

**Asymmetry Dynamics:**
- Georgia registry provides NO federal protection
- Positive THC test triggers federal employment action
- Employee cannot assert state law defense
- Termination proceedings initiated

**Outcome:** Employee terminated; no legal recourse under federal employment law

**Asymmetry Lesson:** Federal-state asymmetry is absolute for federal employees/contractors.

---

### Case Study 4: Dispensary Location Conflict

**Scenario:** Licensed dispensary faces local zoning challenge after state approval.

**Asymmetry Dynamics:**
- GAMCC license issued
- Local zoning authority challenges location
- GAMCC cannot override local zoning
- Investment at risk from local politics
- Limited location alternatives statewide

**Outcome:** Dispensary relocated after 18-month delay; significant cost overruns

**Asymmetry Lesson:** State license ≠ local permission; local politics critical.

## 11. Conclusion

### Key Takeaways

1. **Georgia is fundamentally different** — Not comparable to legalized states
2. **Criminal risk is real** — Any compliance failure has serious consequences
3. **Binary compliance** — Little middle ground between legal and criminal
4. **Federal risk unchanged** — State program provides no federal protection
5. **Legislative dependency** — Program expansion requires political action

### The Georgia ORA Mindset

Operators in Georgia's restricted market must internalize that they operate on the knife's edge between protected activity and criminal exposure. The asymmetries here are not merely regulatory inconveniences—they are existential business risks. Success requires ultra-conservative compliance, obsessive documentation, political engagement, and acceptance that the program may or may not expand based entirely on legislative dynamics beyond operator control.
